A month ago my first child was born.

If you had asked me last year how I was going to keep my career moving while being present for this, I wouldn't have had an answer. What I found, unexpectedly, was a field I didn't know existed. One that let me work remotely on my own schedule, contribute to something that genuinely matters, and be in the room for the moments that don't come back.

That field is AI evaluation and data annotation, and almost nobody outside of it knows it exists.

## The Mental Image Most People Have

When people hear that AI systems like ChatGPT or Claude are built with human input, they picture researchers. PhDs. People who understand the math behind transformer architectures and can read a paper from arXiv over their morning coffee.

These people exist and they're important. But they make up a small fraction of the human labor that goes into building and improving AI systems. Underneath the research layer, there's an enormous amount of work that requires something different — not technical credentials, but judgment. Consistency. Domain knowledge. The ability to read carefully, apply a standard rigorously, and make defensible calls on cases that don't have obvious answers.

That work is called data annotation and AI evaluation. It's invisible to most people. And it's one of the most accessible paths into meaningful AI work that exists right now.

## What the Work Actually Is

Here's what I do on a given day. I'm given a prompt (something a user might ask an AI) and one or more model responses. My job is to evaluate those responses against a detailed rubric.

Sometimes I'm ranking two responses against each other. Sometimes I'm writing a better response from scratch to show the model what good looks like. Sometimes I'm doing the opposite — designing prompts specifically intended to make the model fail, probing for edge cases and inconsistencies before they make it into a product that millions of people use.

None of that requires a computer science degree. It requires the ability to read carefully, think critically, and apply a standard consistently — even when the task is ambiguous, especially when it is.

## The Skills You Already Have

I came to this work from warehouse operations. Even though on the surface it couldn't be more different, the quality control skills I've developed are exactly the same. Can you recognize the difference between something that is right and something that just looks right? That question is the whole job. Whether you're inspecting a shipment before it goes to a customer or evaluating an AI response, your success directly correlates to things like attention to detail and standardized methodology.

Think about what QC muscle you've already built in whatever you've done before:

- **Customer service** — you understand what a helpful, clear response looks like from the user's side. That's directly applicable to evaluating whether a model is actually serving the person who asked.
- **Teaching or tutoring** — you can assess whether an explanation is accurate, appropriately paced, and genuinely understandable. Huge for evaluating educational AI content.
- **Legal, medical, or technical domain work** — you can catch errors and reasoning failures that a generalist annotator would miss entirely. Platforms specifically seek out domain experts for high-value tasks.
- **Writing or editing** — you have an eye for tone, clarity, and whether something says what it means. Essential for evaluating model outputs at the language level.
- **Any QC or operations background** — you're trained to apply standards consistently under volume and time pressure, flag edge cases, and document your reasoning. That's exactly what this work demands.

The AI industry doesn't have a shortage of researchers. It has a shortage of people who can make consistent, high-quality human judgments at scale. That's a different problem, and it's one that almost any professional background can contribute to solving.

## The Path Is Real, But It's Worth Being Honest About

Gig platforms like [DataAnnotation.tech](https://www.dataannotation.tech), Outlier, and Surge are accessible but competitive. The pay at the basic labeling tier reflects that. I'm not going to tell you this is an easy path to a six-figure remote job, because that's not what it is at the start.

What it is: a *real* on-ramp. A way to build demonstrated experience in a field that's growing fast, develop a portfolio of work that hiring managers in AI can actually evaluate, and learn the vocabulary and methods of a discipline from the inside. The gap between gig annotator and salaried AI evaluation specialist is real, but it's crossable — and it's crossable faster if you approach the work deliberately rather than just completing tasks.

For me, the immediate value was different. It was being able to keep working, keep building something, and still be there when my kid needs me. Not every career move is purely about trajectory. Sometimes it's about what the work makes possible in the rest of your life.

## If You're Wondering Whether This Could Be for You

The first step is simpler than you'd expect. DataAnnotation.tech, Outlier, and Alignerr all have open applications. The onboarding usually involves a skills assessment — reading comprehension, attention to detail, sometimes domain-specific tests if you're applying for specialized work. If you pass, you're in the pool.

Most people don't make it this far, and many of those who do aren't doing anything more than earning a little side cash. The differentiation happens through the quality and consistency of your work, and increasingly through what you build around it — a portfolio that shows you understand the field you're contributing to, not just the tasks you're completing.

The invisible job is real. The door is open. And depending on where you're coming from — and what else you need your work to make room for — it might be worth walking through.
